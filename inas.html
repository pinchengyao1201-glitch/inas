Phase One

Choose
Your Sector

The stock market is organized into 11 sectors — broad categories that group companies by what they produce or sell. Technology. Healthcare. Energy. Financials. Utilities. Consumer Staples. Consumer Discretionary. Industrials. Materials. Real Estate. Communication Services.

Your first move isn't to find a stock. It's to choose where in the economy you want to hunt. This shapes everything. Sectors behave differently depending on where we are in the economic cycle — and that means a great company in the wrong sector can still be a losing investment.

When interest rates rise sharply, Utilities and Real Estate suffer because their debt costs climb. When the economy is expanding, Industrials and Consumer Discretionary tend to outperform. When recession fears spike, Healthcare and Consumer Staples hold steady because people still need medicine and groceries no matter what.

Why this comes first

Most beginners jump straight to a company they've heard about. INAS forces you to understand the context your company lives in before you ever look at its ticker. Context is the thing that separates a confident thesis from a lucky guess.

Phase Two

Read the Macro
Environment

The macroeconomic environment is the weather system your company operates in. You cannot change the weather. But if you ignore it, you'll be caught unprepared. There are five dimensions every investor needs to track before they look at any company.

These aren't abstract stats. Each one has a direct mechanism that connects to your company's earnings, its valuation multiple, or both. Understanding these connections is what separates serious analysis from surface-level research.

Why Tech investors should care about interest rates

When rates are high, analysts discount future earnings more aggressively. This is why high-growth Tech companies — whose value depends on earnings years from now — often suffer more than mature dividend-paying companies when rates rise. Macro awareness helps you time your entries.

A sector is a neighborhood. An industry is a specific block inside it. Technology alone contains semiconductors, enterprise software, consumer hardware, IT services, internet platforms, and cloud infrastructure — each with completely different revenue models and risk profiles.

Semiconductors are deeply cyclical — they boom when chip demand spikes and crash when inventory builds up. Enterprise software is stickier — companies pay annual subscriptions and switching costs are high. Consumer hardware follows device replacement cycles. Each industry plays by different rules and responds differently to macro conditions.

When you separate into industries, you're asking a sharper question: which sub-arena has the structural dynamics I want to invest in? High and stable margins? Rapid growth? Resilience in downturns? Each industry answers those differently.

The zoom-in principle

Saying "I invest in Tech" is like saying "I live on Earth." True, but it tells you almost nothing. The industry level is where real decisions start — because that's where unit economics, margin structures, and competitive dynamics become specific enough to compare.

Now you're inside the company. This phase combines quantitative analysis (financial metrics) with qualitative analysis (the SWOT framework). Neither is enough alone — a company with great metrics but no competitive moat can deteriorate, while a company with a strong moat and temporarily weak metrics can be an excellent buy.

How to read these numbers

A P/E of 37.5× means you're paying $37.50 for every $1 of earnings. That's a premium — which is justified only if the company keeps growing earnings. A gross margin of 68.8% means Microsoft keeps $0.69 of every revenue dollar before paying for operations. That's exceptional for a company at this scale. ROIC of 22.9% means management is generating strong returns on the capital they deploy.

Phase Five

Map the
Competitor Network

Every company exists inside a network of rivals, potential entrants, and substitutes. Analyzing a company in isolation — without understanding who it's fighting and where — is like evaluating an athlete without watching their games.

The competitor network phase asks you to identify the main rivals in each business segment, then compare them on the metrics that actually determine winners: market share trajectory, margin structure, growth rate, and strategic positioning.

This isn't just "who's bigger." It's about understanding where your company wins and why, whether that advantage is durable, and whether the competitive dynamics are stable or shifting. A company leading a segment where the rules are changing may be weaker than its current metrics suggest.

You also need to think about non-obvious competition — companies that don't yet compete directly but could enter (potential entrants), and products that serve the same underlying need differently (substitutes). These are where market-share surprises come from.

The most commonly missed risk

Investor theses often assume current market share is permanent. The competitor network phase forces you to actively stress-test whether your company's position is durable — or if it's quietly under threat from a direction nobody is watching.

Phase Six

Portfolio
Allocation

The final phase is where individual analysis becomes a portfolio decision. You've run INAS across multiple sectors and companies. Now you decide: how much weight does each position deserve?

Allocation is about translating conviction into position sizing. A company you understand deeply, with a clear thesis and limited downside risk, might deserve 15–20% of your portfolio. A company with an exciting story but higher uncertainty might deserve 5%. Treating every position equally ignores all the analysis you just did.

Good portfolio construction also requires thinking about correlation. If you hold three Tech companies and one Energy company, you're not really diversified — when Tech has a bad quarter, your whole portfolio suffers together. INAS is designed to guide you through multiple sectors precisely so you can allocate across uncorrelated exposures.

The point of all six phases

Each phase builds the context for the next. By Phase 6, your allocation is grounded in macro awareness, industry dynamics, company fundamentals, and competitive reality — not a gut feeling or a headline. That discipline is what INAS is for.
